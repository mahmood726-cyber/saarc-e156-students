Why It Matters

Government-funded trials in SAARC represent a small fraction of the total, meaning research agendas are largely set by foreign sponsors whose priorities may not align with national health needs.

The Evidence   122 words · target 156
Who controls and benefits from clinical research across the SAARC nations, and does government-funded vs external expose structural inequity in South Asian research? This cross-sectional audit evaluated 11,116 South Asian trials registered on ClinicalTrials.gov across 8 SAARC nations. Investigators computed the chi-squared test of distributional uniformity as the primary estimand using registry metadata. The distribution yielded a Gini coefficient of 0.711, indicating severe concentration of trials among few nations. The Theil index of 0.918 confirmed between-country inequality, with most disparity arising from inter-regional gaps. These findings demonstrate that structural governance deficits perpetuate research dependency and limit South Asian sovereignty over clinical evidence. Interpretation is limited by the use of a single registry and the absence of national trial databases from SAARC nations.
