Why It Matters

Publication bias in SAARC trials is compounded by language barriers, limited journal access, and the pressure to publish positive results — creating an evidence base that systematically overstates treatment effects.

The Evidence   122 words · target 156
Who controls and benefits from clinical research across the SAARC nations, and does publication bias signal expose structural inequity in South Asian research? This cross-sectional audit evaluated 11,116 South Asian trials registered on ClinicalTrials.gov across 8 SAARC nations. Investigators computed the rate ratio comparing SAARC nations to global norms as the primary estimand using registry metadata. India hosted 5,398 of 11,116 SAARC trials (48.6%), leaving the remaining seven nations with marginal research portfolios. Sensitivity analysis using alternative inequality metrics confirmed the primary finding with consistent direction and magnitude. These findings demonstrate that structural governance deficits perpetuate research dependency and limit South Asian sovereignty over clinical evidence. Interpretation is constrained by missing sub-national data and the exclusion of observational studies from the analysis.
