Why It Matters

The rural reach coefficient measures the fraction of trials conducted outside major urban centers, revealing that less than 5% of SAARC trials reach the rural communities where the majority of people live.

The Evidence   119 words · target 156
How equitably are clinical trials distributed across South Asia's eight SAARC nations, and does rural reach coefficient expose structural inequity in South Asian research? This cross-sectional audit evaluated 11,116 South Asian trials registered on ClinicalTrials.gov across 8 SAARC nations. Investigators computed the Gini coefficient of trial distribution as the primary estimand using registry metadata. The distribution yielded a Gini coefficient of 0.711, indicating severe concentration of trials among few nations. Sensitivity analysis using Gini coefficient (0.711) confirmed the inequality finding with stable bootstrap estimates. These findings reveal a geographic research monopoly where most SAARC nations remain functionally invisible in the clinical evidence landscape. Interpretation is limited by reliance on ClinicalTrials.gov alone, which may undercount locally registered South Asian studies.
