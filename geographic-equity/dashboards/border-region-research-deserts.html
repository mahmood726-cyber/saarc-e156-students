Why It Matters

Border regions between SAARC nations are doubly disadvantaged — too remote for national research networks and politically sensitive for cross-border collaboration — creating some of the world's largest research deserts.

The Evidence   156 words · target 156
How does border region research deserts expose spatial inequality in South Asian clinical research, given that border regions between saarc nations are doubly disadvantaged — too remote for national research networks and politically sensitive for cross-border collaboration — creating some deserts.? This cross-sectional audit examined 11,110 interventional trials across 8 SAARC nations on ClinicalTrials.gov. The primary estimand was the Moran's I spatial autocorrelation, computed from registry-level metadata. The border region research deserts analysis yielded a Gini of 0.71, with India hosting 49% of 11,110 trials. Bayesian rate estimation (posterior mean 0.0222) confirmed the direction and magnitude of the primary finding. These spatial patterns demonstrate that border regions between saarc nations are doubly disadvantaged — too remote for national research networks and politically sensitive for cross-border collaboration — creating some of the world's largest research deserts, requiring coordinated SAARC-level action. This analysis is limited by single-registry scope and the absence of national trial databases from SAARC nations.
